The Major Spirits

Every category has its own character. Tap any card to learn more.

🥃

Whisky / Whiskey

Scotland · Ireland · USA · Japan · Canada

The king of aged spirits. Made from fermented grain mash, aged in oak barrels. The spelling "whisky" (no e) = Scotch, Japanese, Canadian. "Whiskey" (with e) = Irish, American.

Aged Complex Oak-forward 40–68% ABV
▶ Expand
Key Styles:

Scotch Single Malt — 100% malted barley, single distillery, aged ≥3 yrs in Scotland. The prestige pinnacle. Regions matter: Islay = smoky/peaty, Speyside = fruity/elegant, Highlands = varied, Lowlands = light.

Bourbon — American, ≥51% corn, new charred oak barrels. Sweeter, vanilla-forward, caramel. No minimum age, but "straight" = ≥2 yrs. Kentucky is home base.

Rye Whiskey — ≥51% rye grain. Spicier, drier than bourbon. Big in cocktails.

Japanese Whisky — Inspired by Scotch, often blended for balance and elegance. Yamazaki, Hibiki, Nikka are the big names.

Irish Whiskey — Triple-distilled, lighter and smooth. Jameson is entry-level; Redbreast single pot still is the good stuff.
🌵

Tequila & Mezcal

Mexico — Jalisco & Oaxaca

Both made from agave plants. Tequila = blue agave only, from Jalisco. Mezcal = any agave variety, traditionally roasted = smoky character. Craft mezcal is having a major moment.

Agave Terroir Earthy 38–55% ABV
▶ Expand
Tequila Age Classifications:

Blanco / Silver — Unaged or ≤60 days. Purest agave flavor. Best for sipping to understand the plant.

Reposado — Aged 2–12 months in oak. Balance of agave + wood.

Añejo — 1–3 years aged. Richer, more complex. Whisky drinkers often gravitate here.

Extra Añejo — 3+ years. Rare, expensive, almost brandy-like.

💡 Flex: The agave plant takes 7–25 years to mature before harvest. One plant = one batch.
🍇

Cognac & Brandy

France — Cognac AOC

Brandy = distilled wine. Cognac = brandy from the Cognac region of France, double-distilled in copper pot stills, aged in Limousin oak. The most regulated spirit in France.

Grape-based Refined Aged 40–43% ABV
▶ Expand
Age Designations:

VS (Very Special) — Youngest brandy in blend ≥2 yrs. Entry level.

VSOP (Very Superior Old Pale) — Youngest ≥4 yrs. The sweet spot for value.

XO (Extra Old) — Youngest ≥10 yrs. Complex, layered, expensive.

Hors d'âge — "Beyond age" — 30, 50, even 100-year blends.

Big houses: Rémy Martin, Hennessy, Courvoisier, Martell. Boutique: Ferrand, Camus.
🫙

Rum

Caribbean · Latin America

Made from sugarcane juice or molasses. Hugely varied — from light Cuban-style to funky Jamaican to aged Barbadian. The most underrated premium spirit category.

Sugarcane Tropical Varied 37–75% ABV
▶ Expand
Styles to Know:

Agricole — From fresh sugarcane juice, not molasses. Grassy, vegetal, funky. Primarily Martinique & Guadeloupe.

Jamaican — High ester, funky, overripe fruit. Appleton, Hampden, Worthy Park.

Barbadian — Elegant, balanced. Mount Gay is the oldest rum brand (1703).

Aged Demerara — From Guyana, deep and heavy. El Dorado 15/21 yr are stunning and affordable vs. whisky.

💡 Hot take: A 15-year aged Barbados rum often rivals $150 Scotch at a fraction of the price.
🌿

Gin

England · Netherlands · Global

A neutral spirit redistilled with botanicals — juniper must be dominant. The flavor profile beyond that is the distiller's art. From London Dry's sharp bite to contemporary floral styles.

Botanical Juniper Aromatic 37–57% ABV
▶ Expand
Key Styles:

London Dry — Classic. Juniper-forward, dry, no added sugar. Tanqueray, Beefeater, Bombay Sapphire.

Old Tom — Slightly sweeter, historic style. Good for classic cocktails.

Navy Strength — 57% ABV. Historically for the Royal Navy. Bold, punchy.

Contemporary / New Western — Juniper present but other botanicals take center stage. Hendrick's (cucumber/rose), Monkey 47 (47 botanicals).
🫗

Vodka

Russia · Poland · Global

Defined by what it's not: no distinctive aroma, taste, or color. Yet base material matters — potato, wheat, rye, and corn all yield different mouthfeels and subtle character.

Neutral Clean Versatile 37.5–50% ABV
▶ Expand
Base Materials:

Grain (wheat/rye) — Crisp, slightly sweet. Grey Goose (wheat), Belvedere (rye).

Potato — Creamy, fuller mouthfeel. Chopin (Poland) is the benchmark.

Corn — Slightly sweet. Tito's (Austin, TX) is huge in the US.

💡 Real talk: Among serious spirits folks, vodka gets least attention. But Polish and Russian traditional vodkas, sipped at room temp with food, are a legit tradition. Don't dismiss it.
Also Worth Knowing

Armagnac

France's other great brandy, from Gascony. Older than Cognac. Single-distillation = more rustic, individual character. Vintages are dated (like wine). A 1980 Armagnac at a bar is a real conversation piece.

Calvados

Apple (and sometimes pear) brandy from Normandy, France. Aged in oak. Tastes like apples transformed — not like apple juice, but deep, dried-fruit complexity. Underappreciated and often stunning.

Pisco

Grape brandy from Peru and Chile. Unaged, distilled once, bottled at natural proof. Clean and fresh but with real depth. The Pisco Sour is the entry point; sipping a premium Quebranta pisco is the revelation.

Amaro / Bitters

Italian herbal liqueurs, bitter-sweet. Fernet-Branca, Campari, Aperol, Cynar, Averna. Not really sipped neat at a fine spirits bar, but knowing Amaro is part of the culture — especially as digestifs after meals.

How to Actually Taste

The difference between drinking and tasting is attention. Here's the protocol.

  1. Observe the Color

    Hold to light. Pale gold = younger or lighter wood. Deep amber/mahogany = longer aging or darker casks (sherry, port). Color is your first clue. Note: caramel coloring is legal in most Scotch and some spirits — color ≠ always age.

  2. Nose It (the Right Way)

    Don't shove your nose in the glass. Hold it an inch or two away. Breathe with your mouth slightly open — it prevents the alcohol from numbing your nose. Give it 3–5 slow passes before forming an opinion. What do you smell first? What comes after? A good spirit has layers.

  3. Add a Few Drops of Water

    Even if you're sipping neat, a few drops of still (non-mineral) water opens up aromatics and cuts the ethanol sting. This is not a sign of weakness — Master Blenders do it. It unlocks esters and lighter floral/fruit notes hidden under the ABV.

  4. The First Sip

    Small sip. Let it coat your tongue. Notice where you feel it: tip = sweet, sides = sour/salt, back = bitter. Roll it around. You're mapping the "mouthfeel" — is it oily? Thin? Astringent? Silky?

  5. The Finish

    After you swallow, how long do the flavors linger? A "long finish" is a quality indicator. What changes? Often warmth, then dried fruit, then spice or oak. Short finish isn't bad — it can mean clean and refreshing. Long finish = complexity.

  6. Return to the Nose

    Come back to the glass 5–10 minutes later. The spirit has oxidized slightly and released different notes. This "second nosing" often reveals things the first didn't. Top-tier spirits can evolve in the glass for an hour.

Flavor Families

Fruits

  • Stone fruit (peach, apricot, plum) — often Speyside Scotch
  • Citrus (orange peel, lemon) — Bourbon, Irish
  • Tropical (banana, pineapple) — Jamaican rum, some Japanese
  • Dried fruit (raisin, fig, prune) — sherry-cask aged spirits
  • Orchard fruit (apple, pear) — Calvados, some Irish

Oak & Baking

  • Vanilla — new American oak (bourbon, very common)
  • Caramel / toffee — charred barrels
  • Cinnamon / nutmeg — rye, some Cognac
  • Dark chocolate — heavily aged spirits
  • Toasted bread / biscuit — malted barley character

Earthy & Savory

  • Peat / smoke — Islay Scotch (Laphroaig, Ardbeg)
  • Leather — long-aged whisky, aged rum
  • Tobacco — XO Cognac, aged Armagnac
  • Roasted agave / earth — mezcal
  • Mushroom / forest floor — very old spirits

Floral & Herbal

  • Heather / honey — Highland Scotch
  • Rose / lavender — Hendrick's Gin, some Armagnac
  • Grass / hay — Agricole rum, blanco tequila
  • Mint / eucalyptus — some bourbon
  • Anise / licorice — Pastis, some Cognac finishes
"The most important thing in tasting is to trust what you smell and taste, not what you're supposed to smell and taste." — Michael Jackson, Whisky Writer

Aging & The Cask

The barrel isn't just storage — it's an active ingredient. Up to 70% of a whisky's flavor can come from the wood.

What Happens in the Barrel

Four simultaneous processes occur during aging:

  • Extraction — Spirit pulls compounds from the wood: vanillin (vanilla), tannins (dryness/structure), lignin (spice), lactones (coconut, oak)
  • Oxidation — Slow exposure to oxygen through the wood softens harsh ethanol, develops dried fruit and nutty notes
  • Evaporation — The "Angel's Share": 1–5% lost per year. Concentrates flavor. Hotter climates lose more (Caribbean rum ages faster)
  • Filtration — Charcoal inside charred barrels removes sulfur compounds, smooths the spirit
Cask Types

American White Oak (New)

Required for bourbon. Heavy vanilla, caramel, sweet coconut. The most impactful oak type. A spirit "first-fill" in new American oak will be dramatically different from "second-fill."

Sherry Casks (Oloroso/PX)

Used by Scotch producers. Adds dried fruit, raisins, Christmas cake, rich mahogany color. Oloroso = dry nutty sherry influence. PX (Pedro Ximénez) = intensely sweet. Among the most prized casks.

Port / Wine Casks

Portuguese port barrels add berry, grape, and chocolate notes. Often used as "finishing" casks — spirit spends final months here. Glenfarclas 105, Glenmorangie Quinta Ruban.

French Limousin Oak

Standard for Cognac and Armagnac. Tighter grain than American oak = slower extraction. More subtle spice, floral notes, and a particular elegance. Doesn't dominate like new American oak.

Age Statements vs. No Age Statement (NAS)

Age Statements

The number on the label = minimum age of any whisky in the bottle. A 12-year contains no whisky younger than 12 years; much of it may be older. The age tells you something — but not everything. A 12-year in the right cask can beat an 18-year in a tired one.

No Age Statement (NAS)

Allows blenders flexibility to use younger and older stocks together for a consistent flavor profile. Not inherently worse — Johnnie Walker Blue, Hibiki Harmony, and Glenfarclas 105 are NAS and exceptional. Judge by taste, not the number.

The "Double Matured" / Finishing Trend

Many distilleries now "finish" their standard expression in a different cask for the last 6–24 months. Glenmorangie pioneered this. Examples: 10-year bourbon-cask Scotch finished in Sauternes (dessert wine) casks. This is where a lot of the creativity in modern whisky lives — and it's a great conversation topic with enthusiasts.

The Glass Matters

Shape directs aroma. Volume controls dilution. The right glass makes a measurable difference.

🥃

Glencairn

The standard for Scotch & whisky tasting. Tulip shape concentrates aroma at the rim. What every serious whisky bar will default to. If they hand you one, you're in a good place.

🍷

Copita / Dock Glass

Long stem, tulip bowl. Professional tasting glass for Cognac and Scotch. Stem keeps hands from warming the spirit.

🥂

Snifter

The classic Cognac glass. Wide bowl, narrow rim. Allows swirling to release aromatics. Don't warm it with your hands — that's outdated advice that ruins the nose.

🧊

Rocks / Old Fashioned

Short, wide, heavy-bottomed. For spirits over ice or cocktails like the Old Fashioned. Casual. The wide opening makes nosing harder — designed for ice and dilution, not tasting.

🍸

Coupe

Broad, shallow bowl. Cocktails like the Sidecar (Cognac-based) or anything served "up" (no ice). Retro-elegant. Replaced the Martini V-glass in most upscale bars.

🫗

Highball

Tall, straight-sided. For highball cocktails — Japanese whisky with soda water is a classic. Ice fills it; carbonation is preserved by the narrow top.

Ice

Large Format Ice ("King Cube")

2x2" cube or large sphere. Melts slower = less dilution. The premium choice for serious spirits on the rocks. If a bar has it, they care. A sphere is optimal (maximum volume to surface area ratio).

Neat vs. Rocks vs. Water

Neat = no ice, room temp. Rocks = on ice. Up = chilled, strained, no ice in the glass. With water = a few drops still water. For tasting fine spirits, neat or neat with a few drops is ideal.

The Vocabulary

The words that come up constantly. Know these and you'll follow any conversation.

Angel's Share

Evaporation from the cask during aging — absorbed into the atmosphere. Typically 1–2% per year in Scotland, up to 5–10% in tropical climates. A 30-year Scotch may have lost 40% of its original volume.

Devil's Cut

Spirit absorbed into the wood stave that doesn't evaporate. Jim Beam trademarked this term but the concept applies broadly. Older barrels have given up more to both angels and devils.

Cask Strength / Barrel Proof

Bottled at whatever ABV it came out of the barrel — no dilution. Can be 55–70%+ ABV. More intense, and purists love it. Add water yourself to suit your palate.

Single Malt vs. Blended

Single malt = one distillery, malted barley. Blended = multiple distilleries combined. Blends aren't inferior — Johnnie Walker, Chivas, etc. are intentionally complex. Many masters argue great blending is as sophisticated as single malts.

Terroir

Borrowed from wine — the idea that soil, climate, water, and local conditions influence flavor. Increasingly used in whisky (peat source, local barley) and mezcal (specific agave varieties, region, altitude) conversations.

Expression

A specific bottling of a spirit. Macallan 12 and Macallan 18 are different expressions from the same distillery. A distillery might have dozens of expressions (age, cask type, region of cask).

Mouthfeel / Body

The texture and weight of the spirit on the palate. "Full-bodied" = rich, coating. "Light-bodied" = thin, watery. Oily mouthfeel (like Clynelish Scotch) is considered a quality marker in many traditions.

Distillate / New Make

The spirit directly off the still, before any aging. Clear, harsh, often surprisingly flavorful. Distilleries sometimes sell it. It's how you taste the raw material before wood transforms it.

Peated / PPM

Peat is partially decomposed plant matter burned to dry malted barley in some Scotch. PPM = phenol parts per million, the measure of peatiness. Laphroaig ≈ 40–45 PPM. Ardbeg ≈ 55 PPM. "Heavily peated" means medicinal, smoky, maritime character.

Vintage vs. Non-Vintage

Most spirits are non-vintage — blended across years for consistency. Some Cognac houses and Scotch bottlers release single-vintage expressions (e.g., Armagnac 1985). These are more like wine — tied to one harvest year's conditions.

Holding Your Own

Smart questions and things to say when you're at the bar with people who know their stuff.

When someone orders a specific Scotch
"Is that a sherry-cask or bourbon-cask expression?"
Almost every enthusiast has an opinion on this. Sherry-cask drinkers tend to love dried fruit and richness; bourbon-cask people want that vanilla and lighter fruit. Asking this shows you understand the core variable in Scotch production.
When someone orders Mezcal
"What agave variety is that? Espadín or something more obscure?"
Espadín is the workhorse agave for mezcal (like the blue agave of tequila). Tobalá, Arroqueño, Tepeztate are wild-harvested varieties — rarer and more expensive. This question signals you understand mezcal is a category, not just a brand.
When ordering a whisky you've never had
"What's the cask story on this one?"
Bartenders at fine spirits bars love this question. It opens up conversation about first-fill vs. refill, finishing casks, cooperage. You'll learn something and signal you're there to appreciate, not just consume.
When someone shows off a rare bottle
"What's the ABV on that — is it cask strength?"
This shows you understand that cask strength releases are the connoisseur's choice — undiluted, unadulterated. If it is, follow with: "Do you add any water to open it up?" Great conversation starter.
Universal order for a fine spirits bar (when unsure)
Ask: "What's a well-aged single malt you're proud of right now — something interesting, not the usual 12-year?"
This invites the bartender to be an expert, shows you have some knowledge, and gets you something genuinely interesting. It's never the wrong move. If they light up, you're in for something good.
When you can't smell or taste what others are describing
Just say: "I'm still training my palate on this one."
Honest and respected. The palate genuinely does develop with exposure. The first 5–10 times you nose a peated Scotch, you may only get smoke. Eventually you start to notice the brine, medicinal notes, peat type. It's real — not pretension. Acknowledging you're learning is the right move.
The Golden Rule
"There are no wrong answers in tasting — only your honest perception. The vocabulary is just a shared language for describing what you already experience."

Practical Order of Learning

  • Start: A Speyside Scotch (Glenfiddich 12, Glenlivet 12) — the approachable baseline
  • Then: A bourbon (Buffalo Trace, Maker's Mark) — compare vanilla/sweetness vs. Scotch
  • Then: A sherry-cask Scotch (Glenfarclas 12, Aberlour 12) — taste what oak type does
  • Then: An Islay Scotch (Laphroaig 10 or Bowmore 12) — smoke, peat, maritime
  • Then: A VS or VSOP Cognac (Rémy Martin VSOP) — grape spirit, flower and fruit
  • Then: An añejo tequila (Fortaleza, El Tesoro) — how aged agave differs from grain
