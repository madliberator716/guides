Kitchen

Countertops & Backsplash

Combination Tool (nozzle side)Auto mode

The wide nozzle sweeps crumbs and flour off flat surfaces quickly without scattering them. Flip to the brush side for loose debris near the backsplash edge or stove knob area.

Pro TipVacuum before wiping — you'll cut your counter-cleaning time in half by pulling up loose debris first.

Stove Drip Pans & Burner Grates

Stubborn Dirt BrushBoost mode

Stiff bristles agitate dried, baked-on food particles from cast-iron grates and drip pan edges that suction alone won't lift. Use before a deep clean to pull up the loose layer first.

Pro TipShort scrubbing strokes in one direction — back and forth just pushes debris around.

Refrigerator Coils & Behind Appliances

Crevice Tool + Extension HoseAuto mode

Dust buildup on fridge coils forces the compressor to work harder. The crevice tool with extension hose reaches the narrow gap behind and below the fridge without pulling it out from the wall.

Pro TipDo this twice a year — clean coils can reduce energy use by up to 25%.

Cabinet & Drawer Interiors

Crevice ToolAuto mode

The crevice tool gets into shelf corners and drawer channels where crumbs and spices collect. Faster and cleaner than wiping, especially in deep cabinets.

Pro TipEmpty the drawer fully first — vacuuming around items just repositions debris.

Range Hood Filters & Vents

Mini Soft Dusting BrushAuto mode

Soft bristles loosen grease-dusty buildup from vent slats and range hood filters without bending the metal fins. Use before washing the filter — removes the dry surface layer first.

High Shelves & Top of Cabinets

Up-Top Adaptor + Mini Soft Dusting BrushAuto mode

Kitchen grease combines with dust on top of cabinets to create a sticky film. The up-top adaptor + soft brush reaches these surfaces without a step stool, and catches the dust rather than knocking it off.

Pro TipLay a sheet of wax paper on top of cabinets after cleaning — makes the next cleanup a one-second swap.

Baseboards

Combination Tool (brush side)Auto mode

Kitchen baseboards accumulate grease-coated dust that sticks and builds up fast near the stove and sink. The brush side of the combination tool loosens and lifts it without scratching the paint.

Pro TipVacuum baseboards before mopping the floor — otherwise mop water just pushes baseboard grime onto the clean floor.
Bathroom

Tile Grout Lines

Stubborn Dirt BrushBoost mode

Stiff bristles fit into grout channels to break up trapped soap scum, hair, and dry debris. Run it along grout lines before mopping — loosens buildup that a wet mop just smears around.

Pro TipUse in Boost mode and work in one direction along each grout line for maximum pickup.

Vanity Surfaces & Mirror Frame

Scratch-Free Dusting BrushAuto mode

The soft, scratch-free bristles are safe on glossy counters, mirror edges, and decorative fixtures. Picks up makeup dust, hair, and fine powder residue without scratching high-gloss surfaces.

Pro TipPull the rotating cuff back between strokes so the bristles self-clean — keeps makeup pigment from transferring.

Floor-to-Wall Gaps & Toilet Base

Crevice ToolAuto mode

The narrow tip reaches the gap where the toilet base meets the floor and the tight seam between the tub and baseboard — areas a mop skips entirely and hair and grime accumulate fast.

Pro TipDo a dry vacuum pass before mopping — removes the bulk so your mop water stays cleaner longer.

Exhaust Fan Vent Cover

Mini Soft Dusting Brush or Crevice ToolAuto mode

Bathroom exhaust fans clog with lint and dust surprisingly fast, reducing airflow. The soft dusting brush clears the outer slats; the crevice tool pulls debris out of the deeper vent slots.

Pro TipA clean exhaust fan prevents mold — aim to vacuum the vent cover every 1–2 months.

Shower & Tub Surround

Combination Tool (brush side)Auto mode

A dry vacuum pass along the tub ledge and shower surround picks up hair and dry debris before it gets wet and sticks. Much easier than trying to remove hair from a wet surface.

Pro TipRun the vacuum before turning on the shower — wet hair in a drain is significantly harder to remove than dry hair on a surface.

Baseboards

Bathroom baseboards collect hair, dust, and humidity-caked debris right at floor level. The brush side of the combination tool runs along the face and top edge without scuffing paint.

Pro TipAngle the tool so the bristles hit both the face and the top ledge in one pass — saves a second run.
How to Vacuum — Hardwood & Hard Floors

Hardwood, LVP & Tile

Laser Slim Fluffy™ Head · Auto mode
🔦Use the Laser Slim Fluffy™ head. The green laser angle-illuminates microscopic dust and debris invisible to the naked eye — works best in slightly dimmed light.
🔄Use Auto mode. It reads debris density in real time and adjusts suction automatically. Hard floors rarely need Boost — you'll waste battery and scatter fine dust.
↔️Overlap each pass by about half the head width. The soft roller is wide but debris hides at the edge. Overlapping ensures complete coverage.
➡️Vacuum with the grain, not against it. On wood planks, debris collects in the seams running lengthwise. Vacuuming along the grain pulls it out rather than pushing it further in.
🚫Don't press down. The Laser Slim Fluffy glides on the soft roller — pressing creates drag and can leave marks on delicate finishes.
🪟Dim the room slightly for the laser pass. The green laser makes invisible dust fluorescent — it's far more effective in moderate rather than bright light.
🧹Finish edges with the crevice tool. The floor head can't reach where the floor meets the baseboard. A quick pass with the crevice tool captures the debris line that accumulates there.
Pro TipIf the LCD particle graph stays elevated after two passes, you haven't fully covered the area — slow down your pace. The V15 is counting what it picks up in real time.
How to Vacuum — Carpet

Low-Pile & Medium-Pile Carpet

Digital Motorbar™ Head · Auto mode
🔄Use the Digital Motorbar™ head. The motorized brush bar agitates carpet fibers and the DLS™ sensor adjusts brush bar resistance automatically as pile height changes.
🔄Auto mode is the right default. It increases suction when the Piezo sensor detects more debris. You'll see the graph spike on heavy areas and settle as you clear them.
🐢Slow, deliberate strokes outperform fast sweeps. A slow pull — about 1 second per foot — gives the brush bar time to agitate fibers and the suction time to pull debris up.
↔️Go over high-traffic areas twice, in two directions. First north-to-south, then east-to-west. Crossing directions lifts debris from multiple angles in the pile.
🔁Vacuum from the far corner back toward the door. Walking forward onto already-vacuumed carpet redeposits debris. Work backward so you exit over clean carpet.
🧱Edge the perimeter first. Switch to the crevice tool for the carpet-baseboard seam before doing the main field — debris at the edge won't get kicked into your clean pass.
Pro TipWatch the LCD particle count — don't stop until the graph drops to near baseline. That's your signal the area is actually clean, not just visually tidy.

Deep / Thick-Pile & Shag Carpet

Digital Motorbar™ Head · Boost for heavy passes
⚡Use Boost for the first pass on heavy shag. Deep pile holds debris far down in the fibers — Boost's maximum suction pulls it up. Switch back to Auto for follow-up passes to conserve battery.
🐢Go even slower than medium pile. On shag, the head needs extra dwell time on each section. Half-speed strokes make a real difference in what gets picked up.
↔️Three-direction passes on very thick pile. North-south, east-west, and diagonal. Dense fiber traps debris from multiple angles.
🔃Check the anti-tangle comb more often. Long shag fibers wrap around the brush bar faster. Inspect and clear after every session on thick-pile rugs.

Stairs (Carpet-Covered)

Mini Motorized Tool · Auto mode
⬆️Detach the wand and work handheld with the Mini Motorized Tool. The full wand is too long for stair treads — handheld gives you control and maneuverability.
🔽Start at the top and work down. Debris dislodged from upper stairs falls to lower ones — always vacuum top-to-bottom.
↩️Pull toward you on each tread. On stair carpet, pulling (not pushing) lifts the nap and pulls debris out rather than compressing it into the fibers.
📐Don't forget the risers. The vertical face of each stair step collects hair and dust. Run the mini tool up the riser with the same pull motion.
🧱Use the crevice tool for the tread-riser seam. The corner where each tread meets the riser is a debris trap the motorized head can't fully reach.
Pro TipStairs go faster when you sit one step above the one you're cleaning — stable position, full control, no awkward reaching.
How to Vacuum — Rugs & Kitchen Runners

Area Rugs (Low to Medium Pile)

Digital Motorbar™ Head · Auto mode
↩️Vacuum the underside first every few weeks. Flip the rug and vacuum the back to dislodge embedded debris — it falls to the floor and you pick it up when you vacuum the top side.
↔️Vacuum across the fringe, not along it. Running the head along fringe wraps it around the brush bar. Approach from the side, stopping before the fringe, then use the crevice tool along the edge.
🔄Two passes in opposite directions. Same principle as carpet — north-south, then east-west lifts fibers from both directions and extracts more debris.
📌Secure the rug before vacuuming. Thin rugs shift under the suction of the Motorbar. Use a rug pad, or have the far edge against a wall. A moving rug means incomplete passes.
🔃Rotate the rug quarterly. It distributes wear and ensures you're not repeatedly vacuuming the same traffic pattern into the same fibers.

Delicate & Flat-Weave Rugs

Laser Slim Fluffy™ or Combination Tool · Auto mode
⚠️Don't use the Digital Motorbar™ on delicate, hand-woven, or flat-weave rugs. The motorized brush bar can snag loops, pull threads, or damage fragile weaves.
✅Use the Laser Slim Fluffy™ head instead. The soft roller is safe on flat-weave, jute, sisal, and low-loop rugs — gentle contact, strong suction.
🔽Reduce to Auto mode and use light pressure. Let suction do the work. On very delicate rugs, a single slow pass is better than repeated aggressive ones.
🚫Never vacuum fringed edges with a powered head. Always use the crevice tool or combination tool (brush side) along edges with fringe — the brush bar will wrap and pull the fringe immediately.
Pro TipIf you're unsure about a rug's construction, test in a hidden corner with one gentle pass before committing to a full clean.

Kitchen Runners

Digital Motorbar™ or Laser Slim Fluffy™ · Auto mode
🍳Vacuum runners more frequently than other rugs. Kitchen runners sit in the highest foot-traffic zone and near food prep — they accumulate debris, grease particles, and food crumbs faster than any other textile surface.
🔄Choose the head based on pile. Textured anti-fatigue runners → Digital Motorbar™. Flat-weave or low-profile runners → Laser Slim Fluffy™.
📋Vacuum before mopping the kitchen floor. Always do a dry vacuum pass over the runner before any wet cleaning of the surrounding floor — otherwise mop water drags runner debris across the floor.
🔁Shake or flip before vacuuming for embedded crumbs. A quick shake outside (or a flip-and-vacuum-the-back pass) dislodges surface-layer debris that's too heavy for suction alone to pull from dense kitchen mats.
🧱Edge the runner-to-floor seam with the crevice tool. Crumbs and grit accumulate at the border where the runner meets the hard floor — this line is easy to miss with the main head.
⚠️Check for anti-fatigue rubber backing. Thick rubber-backed kitchen mats can resist suction and shift aggressively — hold one edge steady or work from the center outward.
Pro TipKitchen runners near the stove absorb grease vapors over time. If the vacuum starts to smell after vacuuming the runner, it's time to wash it — grease is burning on the motor exhaust.
